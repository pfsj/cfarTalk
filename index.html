Network Structure as Driver of Racial Disparities

Perhaps one of the most well studies network feature implicated in the spread of disease is concurrency. Concurrency indicates if an individual had overlaping sexual partenrs. That is, for example, if you had sex with PARTNER A on monday, sex with PARTNER B on wednesday, and then had sex with PARTNER A again on Friday. Both in modeling and limited real life studies this has been shown to spread disease more quickly than serial monogamy. That is differenty than haveing sex with three different partners on monday, wednesday, and friday. On the dynamic network animation here, concurrency is represented by having a tie to more than a singgle individual at any one time. The general problem of concurrency is that it creates larger connected components of the network which leads disease to spread more quickly through the network. Concurrency is implicated in disparities because if certain groups show higher levels of concurrency then that could be a source of increase disparities, even when engaging in the same level of sex and protective behaviors such as condom use

Network Structure as Driver of Racial Disparities

Very closely related to concurrency is the network charactertic: 'degree'. Degree represnts the number of sex partners an individual has within a certain period of time. Due to the difficulty of measuring longitudinal networks we often examine them cross sectionally (e.g., including sex partners in the past 7 day). In this way, degree is analagous to a non-dynamic cross sectional view of concurrency where concurrency is now represented by any indiovidual that has more than a single tie in the graph. This toy example also is a great representation of the fundamental reason why an individuals risk behavior may not be the best metric for risk of infection. Each of these 5 individuals are not behaving particularly risky because they only have a single sexual partner in the past 7 days. However, their risk of infection also depends on the their partners behavior, and in this case, their partner increases their own risk by having a degree of 5 with multiple concurrent parters. Again, degree may be implicated in racial disparities if it varies across different racial groups

Finally, homophily is the last network characteristic. Homophily represents the idea that birds of a feather flock together. That is, we may be more or less likely to have sex partners that are like ourselves. As you migth imagine, if there is high levels of homophily, it tends to reinforce the existing prevelance of a disease within groups because it is diffuclt for a disease to enter/exit certain groups given that they are more likely to have sex with those in the same group relative to those in a different group. While homophily is not necessarily implicated in the original cause of racial disparities, it might explain how, given the high levels of racial disparities in HIV, how these disparities are maintained over time

Modeling Social Network Data

So, before we dive into the results, we need to figure out how to estimate a model that can examine our hypothesis regarding these network features. Network data has a number of features that complicate it's analysis compared to traditiona social/behavioral data. Foremost among them is that network data is, by defenition non-independence. Today I'll be discussing a class of models called exponential random graph models or commonly called ERGM

Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs)

ERGM are a class of models that focus on predicting which individuals have ties. In that sense, they are essentially just more sophisticated logistic models comparing predictors of nodes that have ties and those that don't have ties. Similarly, the results are relatively straight forward and similar to logistic regression where an estimate is provided for each parameter indicating if the parameter has a an association with the likelihood of a tie being present. However, these models differ from logistic regression in that they not only allow thigns like if nodes with a given characteristic are more likely to have ties (we call these exogenous because they are external to the structure itself) but ERGMs also let you use the structure of the network itself (i.e., endogenous characteristics) to predict if there is a tie. A common example of this is examing if we share a friend, are two nodes share a connection to a third node, are they more or less likely to have a tie themselves.

How ERGMs Work

Although a major oversimplification of the process, ERGM estimation can be simplified into 4 rough steps. First, based on our observed network, we assume a network of exactly that size. So, if we're analyzing a classroom network with 20 children, we assume a network of 20 nodes. Next, we decide what parameters to include in the model. As a simple example let's assume we onyl want to extimate two parameters. The number of connections in the network (this is essentially an interecept parameter) and the level of racial homophily. Then based on those parameters we randomly simulate graphs across the parameters space. Given these sampled simulations, we adjust the parameter estimates to most accurately represent our observed data. Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC)
  1. Assume network size 'n' (with characteristics)
  2. Define parameters
  3. Given parameters, sample from distribution of networks
  4. Find parameters that best match observed data
To make this a little more tangible. On the left side here we have the observed network. As you can see, there is strong homophily by the color of the nodes with many more connections within nodes of the same color versus those of different colors. On the right side we have a random network with the same number of nodes and ties, as well as roughly the same number of nodes of each color. However, the edges are randomly assigned. By simulated many iterations of this 'random' network we can do two things. First, we can modify how we're simulating it to try and make it more closely match the observed network and we can also provide an estimate of how likely the observed parameter is relative to random samples of the same network with other parameters held constant. In this case, when I click on the figure we get a new random network with the edge parameter held constant so we can compare the level of homophily in a random network with the same number/type of nodes and number of edges relative to the level of homophily in our observed network. Obviously, it is very unlikely that our network that we observed had the level of homophily on chance given how dissimilar the random graphs look from our observed network. There are many other reasons that the graph looks disimmilar but I won't go into those right now. Hopefully, this basic example demonstrates how by sampling random graphs we can estimate parameters for the observed network

ERGMs for Ego-centric Network Data

So, if you believe me that ERGMs are an appropriate means to estimate parameters of networks. We have one final challenge that we have to overcome to apply it to the data I'll be discussing today. And this is that ERGMs are designed to assess networks that we completely observe. However, this is rarely possible for sexual networks given that we don't measure the behavior of all people in the network, we merely observe a sample of individual's sexual partners. These personal views of one's network ties is called an 'ego centric' network with the focal individual known as the 'ego' and the partners being known as 'alters'
So, on this slide we can see the same whole network on the left with nodes that we're going to sample being slightly larger than the other nodes. This same graph is represneted on the right assuming the sampled individuals have a perfect perception of the network ties between their partners. Without getting bogged down in the details of how how to use the ERGM approach for sampled egocentric data. The basic idea is that we do the same type of simulation of random graphs to estimate the parameters but instead of having the true observed network, we try and create a 'true' network using the distribution of information provided by these sampled egocentric network. For example, how many connections do green nodes have on average, how many of those connections are to other green nodes, etc. Using this information we estimate how these statistics would manifest in whole network of a given size. We can then examine how closely the converged model represents the distirbution of our egocentric data, most commonly by examining the degree distribution and how closely it fits our data (i.e., how many people have 0 partners, how many have 1 partner, etc.)
